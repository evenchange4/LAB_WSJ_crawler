LOS ANGELES -- A fatal shooting rampage recently raised questions about security at Los Angeles International Airport, but disputes over the facility's fitful path to modernization pose their own set of problems.

Despite a showy new international terminal that opened in September, international traffic at LAX -- as the sprawling hub is known -- is weaker than projected years earlier, while upgrades are still to be made to aging domestic gates and elevators and outdated runway layouts.

Now, the airport also faces political and construction problems that threaten a nearly $5 billion modernization drive.

For Los Angeles and LAX, consistently ranked one of the worst in passenger satisfaction among the top 20 international hubs, the stakes are big. Rival U.S. airports with upgraded facilities are competing hard to siphon off both domestic and international traffic, while a big chunk of the region's economic future is tied to LAX's role as the leading gateway to fast-growing Asian tourism and other markets.

"We're a world-class airport by accident," said Eric Garcetti, Los Angeles's new mayor, in an interview a day before the Nov. 1 shooting. "People need to fly in and out of LAX," he said, but beyond the new terminal, "there's nothing else that's world-class about it."

Erin Cook, a 31-year-old Los Angeles resident who uses the airport at least once a month, echoed many travelers' complaints. LAX "is really crowded, always full of traffic, kind of confusing," said the public-relations professional. She tries to avoid LAX and fly out of nearby Bob Hope Airport in Burbank.

This year, just six years after completion, one of LAX's main runways started cracking, a setback that could slow operations just as the airport tries to get its modernization off the ground. The city is suing the runway's designers and contractors.

Meanwhile, Mr. Garcetti has joined airport neighbors in opposing another runway project sought by U.S. regulators -- and backed by the airport's director -- intended to enhance safety and efficiency.

Critics say the legal and contractual problems could undermine the airport's position as America's primary aerial connection to fast-growing Pacific markets. San Francisco, Seattle and Dallas are among cities eager to attract such traffic.

Gina Marie Lindsey, LAX's top official, said that despite some advances, the airport still lags behind competitors in passenger appeal. "We can't build fast enough" to keep up with those rivals and with traffic growth, said Ms. Lindsey, who is executive director of Los Angeles World Airports.

The 85-year-old facility generates some 300,000 jobs across Los Angeles County, according to the county's Economic Development Corporation. Local and state tax revenue from airport operations and related businesses totals $2.5 billion a year.

LAX, however, took longer than other big U.S. airports to recover from the terror attacks of 2001 and the global recession that followed.

At the lowest point, in 2002, passenger traffic through the international terminal fell to about seven million people from 14 million annually before the attacks, before rising to about nine million today. Overall traffic totals about 66 million passengers a year, still more than 2% below 2000, while international traffic volume between 2009 and 2012 grew more slowly than at five other big U.S. hubs.

The airport has made some upgrades to domestic terminals, and it envisions spending $270 million to replace 3 miles of escalators, walkways and elevator shafts. Indeed, airport managers believed 2013 would be highlighted by the new international terminal -- a 3-acre space featuring giant multimedia screens, boutiques and restaurants.

Amid the excitement of the terminal's opening, however, Ms. Lindsey was told portions of the newly paved southernmost runway showed signs of premature deterioration. The runway remains in use, but LAX officials are considering how much repairs will cost.

Airport officials fault quality-control lapses by contractors for the problems with the $250 million strip. One contractor named in the suit, Tutor-Saliba Corp., which has since merged and is now called Tutor Perini Corp., said the airport's maintenance of the runway is to blame.

Another issue threatens plans to relocate one of two parallel runways across the airfield. Federal Aviation Administration officials say without a new taxiway between the runways -- which are too close to allow one -- poor visibility and traffic congestion raise the risk of pilots rolling planes onto the wrong strip.

The city council last year approved moving one runway 260 feet closer to surrounding neighborhoods. But Mr. Garcetti, then on the council, opposed the idea. A group of labor leaders and neighborhood and municipal officials have sued. "Ninety-nine percent of folks who land at LAX aren't concerned about a runway. They're concerned about crumbling airport terminals," Mr. Garcetti said.
