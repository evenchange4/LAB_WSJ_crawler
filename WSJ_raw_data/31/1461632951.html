    Nasdaq OMX Group Inc. senior executive Eric Noll, once considered a likely successor to the exchange operator's chief executive, quit to take the top position at a brokerage firm.

    Mr. Noll, 51 years old, said he would become chief executive of ConvergEx Group LLC, a broker for institutional traders and money managers.

    The move surprised many industry experts, most of whom had anticipated Mr. Noll would stay at Nasdaq until current Chief Executive Robert Greifeld retired.

    But Mr. Greifeld, 56, has shown no signs of leaving. His current contract, signed last year, lasts until 2017. Under direction from its board, Nasdaq had been working on an executive succession plan for two years, although no details have been made public.

    The company also has been considering outside candidates for the succession plan, according to a person familiar with discussions at Nasdaq.

    Mr. Noll, whose title was executive vice president of U.S. and U.K. transaction services, oversaw much of Nasdaq's technology. Under his direction, Nasdaq in July purchased the electronic Treasurys-trading platform eSpeed for $750 million from BCG Partners Inc. The deal gave Nasdaq more access to the fixed-income market at a time when the exchange's profits are slowing due to declines in stock trading.

    But Mr. Noll also shared responsibility for some high-profile glitches at the exchange, including its mishandling of Facebook Inc.'s 2012 public offering and a recent outage that closed the stock market for several hours.

    Mr. Noll had "generally been looked at as number two in line" for chief executive at Nasdaq, said Richard Repetto, an analyst who covers exchanges at Sandler O'Neill + Partners. "He was very well respected in the industry."

    His move to ConvergEx comes with its own challenges. The firm is under investigation by the Justice Department and the Securities and Exchange Commission for allegedly overcharging clients.

    In an interview Monday, Mr. Noll said he decided to leave Nasdaq after he was approached by ConvergEx's recruiting team.

    "I wasn't looking for a new position but when I was approached to consider this I was immediately interested," he said.

    ConvergEx said in a statement in October that the investigation is centered on "certain customer orders that were executed through the former trading desk of our Bermuda affiliate, ConvergEx Global Markets. Upon learning of this misconduct, we shut down that trading desk in late 2011, and ConvergEx discontinued the activity in question."

    ConvergEx has faced volatility on other fronts, too. In 2011, a deal to sell itself to CVC Capital Partners, a private-equity firm, fell apart. The next year it managed to sell its Eze Castle Software and RealTick businesses to TPG, another private-equity company, in a deal valued at $1.9 billion. In June it withdrew plans to issue stock to the public, but didn't comment on its decision.

    The company's "focus is to move forward," Mr. Noll said. He declined to discuss the details of the ongoing investigations. "We have cooperated completely with regulators and are committed to putting these legacy issues behind us," he said.

    Mr. Noll was named president of ConvergEx and will add the title of CEO by year-end, the brokerage said.

    Mr. Noll's departure comes nearly two weeks after it emerged that another prominent stock-exchange official was leaving his post. Lawrence Leibowitz, chief operating officer of NYSE Euronext, is leaving the exchange at the end of the year, according to people familiar with the changes. The NYSE was formally taken over by Atlanta-based IntercontinentalExchange Group Inc. on Nov. 13.

    The NYSE and Nasdaq Stock Market, long seen as the central powers in U.S. stock investing, are facing rising competition from newer exchanges and alternative trading platforms.

    Mr. Noll was brought to Nasdaq in 2009 to succeed Christopher Concannon, another executive once considered a possible successor to Mr. Greifeld. Mr. Concannon left Nasdaq in 2009 to join Virtu Financial LLC, a high-frequency trading firm.

    Mr. Noll's resignation "signals that the number of internal candidates for succession planning has been dramatically reduced," according to a person familiar with internal discussions.

    "Bob's been there for 11 to 12 years now. I don't know when he's leaving, but it sure seems to be drawing on," the person said.

    Mr. Noll's decision comes three months after a technology glitch forced Nasdaq to halt trading on thousands of stocks for three hours. Mr. Noll also was part of a team of executives who oversaw the exchange's fumbling of Facebook's IPO, which led to a fine and millions of dollars of claims from investors who said they lost money because of the exchange's handling of the IPO.

    On the day of the Facebook IPO, Mr. Noll was in Philadelphia, away from the operations center for the IPO, The Wall Street Journal previously reported.

    A week later, Mr. Noll told the Journal, "If we had known that our solution was inadequate, we would have fixed the issue with the right solution going forward."

    During a conference call with brokers after the Facebook IPO, Mr. Noll said the exchange's early interpretation of problems that delayed trading in Facebook shares by 30 minutes was incorrect. "We thought we had a fix," he said on the call.
