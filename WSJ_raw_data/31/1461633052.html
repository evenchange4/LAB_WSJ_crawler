    The Food and Drug Administration, in a U-turn from its position three years ago, removed restrictions on GlaxoSmithKline PLC's diabetes drug Avandia and said it no longer had serious concerns over the drug's heart-attack risk.

    "Our actions today reflect the most current scientific knowledge about the risks and benefits of this drug," said Janet Woodcock, the agency's drug chief. "Given these new results, our level of concern is considerably reduced; thus, we are requiring the removal of certain prescribing restrictions."

    One prominent Avandia critic, Steven Nissen of the Cleveland Clinic, predicted doctors wouldn't return to prescribing the drug.

    "I do not think this decision is in the public interest," said Dr. Nissen, the clinic's chairman of cardiovascular medicine and a prominent researcher who presented evidence of the drug's risk. "This step is from reanalysis of a clinical trial that was not designed properly in the first place and would not meet modern regulatory standards."

    Currently, only certified doctors are permitted to prescribe Avandia, and they must state that patients have tried and failed on other medicines. Patients must state that they understand the heart risks. The restrictions have limited use of the drug, which had once garnered sales of some $3 billion a year.

    Glaxo has maintained that the drug is a safe and effective treatment for diabetes. Several years ago, the company asked Duke University to review a study known as the Record trial, which the company said showed no increase in heart attacks for Avandia patients. The Duke review, released earlier this year, largely confirmed the company's view.

    In June, an FDA advisory committee called for easing restrictions on the medicine. At the panel meeting, FDA officials strongly endorsed the Duke readjudication as credible and professionally conducted. A majority of panelists said they no longer thought the heart worries were as serious as once believed.

    Glaxo said on Monday that it "welcomes the decision of the FDA and appreciates the agency's robust review of the science with regard to Avandia." The company didn't discuss how aggressively it plans to market the drug now. It said it posted a $19 million loss on Avandia in the U.S. last year because returns of the product outweighed new sales.

    The Record trial, paid for by Glaxo, was "open-label," meaning that patients and doctors knew who got Avandia and who didn't. Such studies aren't favored by most researchers because of possible bias.

    The Record trial contradicted the results of a 2007 analysis of multiple studies by Cleveland Clinic doctors, which showed a 43% heart-attack increase. The Record study, done entirely outside the U.S. and mostly in Europe, failed to confirm that result. It did show a doubling of heart failure in Avandia patients.

    The FDA in September 2010 put limitations on the sale of Avandia. Glaxo added a black-box warning to the Avandia label in November 2007.

    Glaxo last year agreed to plead guilty to criminal charges related to sales of drugs including Avandia and to pay $3 billion to the U.S. government. The guilty plea covered a range of behavior including withholding safety data about Avandia from the FDA. Glaxo said Monday in a statement that the failure to report safety data was an "inadvertent omission."
