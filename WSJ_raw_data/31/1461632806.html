    In the days leading up to the Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade, the cast and crew of "Matilda the Musical" were perfecting their show routine.

    Some concerns lingered: A pile of coats, mistakenly left onstage, nearly tripped an actor; two cast members narrowly avoided colliding during an intricate dance sequence; and the actress playing Matilda grunted at the unexpected weight of a stack of books she is supposed to lift.

    And there were other fears, too.

    "I'm nervous something weird will happen, like a pigeon pooping on my head," said Milly Shapiro, 11 years old, one of the four girls in the Broadway musical's lead role. Her solo will open the Thanksgiving Day number, as the cast entertains frigid spectators stationed at the end of the parade route.

    "Matilda" is one of four Broadway shows performing at Herald Square as part of Thursday's parade. When the annual celebration began in 1924, a handful of bands and animals from the Central Park Zoo marched 6 miles downtown from 145th Street to Herald Square, parade organizers said.

    Since then, it has become a multimedia spectacle, with 28 floats, close to 50 large and midsize balloons and 11 marching bands at last year's parade. Some 3.5 million spectators watched along the route -- now 2.5 miles -- and another 50 million television viewers tuned in as announcers ran through a 200-page script.

    Broadway performances at Herald Square have also grown from modest show-tune renditions to full-scale productions. The numbers are only 3 1/2 minutes -- but they can cost more than $100,000 to produce.

    "It's kind of a pinnacle experience," said Sara Brians, who performed in 2001 as part of the cast of "42nd Street" and now serves "Matilda" as the resident choreographer. "A lot of people in the cast have said, 'This is what I've waited my whole career to do.'"

    Not every Broadway show works for the Thanksgiving parade. "Urinetown," which the producers of "Matilda" also worked on, never got the call. "Matilda," based on a Roald Dahl children's story blending bullying, parental neglect and uplifting child rebellion, was a natural fit, but adapting a snippet of it for the outdoor parade was daunting.

    "That's always the challenge for the choreographers and the music directors," said Bill Schermerhorn, the parade's creative director. "How do you translate what people see in the theater and put it on the street for parade day, in any kind of weather, and have the people out there performing their hearts out?"

    The "Matilda" creative team started from a medley of three songs they performed during the Tony Awards, then trimmed 30 seconds and abandoned props such as the school desks, which were too unstable on 34th Street's uneven ground.

    One afternoon last week, the four Matildas lined up to practice a series of possible entrances before the choreographers scrapped all the variations in favor of a new idea.

    "There are so many versions that sometimes I get confused," said 10-year-old Sophia Gennusa, one of the Matildas. But it didn't dampen her excitement, or the enthusiasm of the other three Matildas, who alternate solos at big events.

    Oona Laurence, 11, grew up in Manhattan watching the parade from a distance. "I never actually got to see it up close because all the seats were taken," she said.

    Now, she'll "literally be in it," she said, eyes wide. "I just can't wait."

    As the routine came together, initial snags like the near-collisions and missed marks smoothed out.

    But there will be additional, on-site challenges: Shows cannot tape marks on the ground, so when cast members practice on the street, they will have to navigate using the Macy's star logo on the ground.

    The weather is always a wild card.

    "If the Rockettes can do it in short dresses, we can certainly do it in socks and shoes and shorts," said Michael David, president of the Dodgers, the production company behind "Matilda."

    One worry the cast won't have to contend with: singing.

    For decades, Thanksgiving-parade performances have been prerecorded, with few exceptions.

    That avoids wind getting into the microphones; signal interference from the millions of cellphones, cameras and electronic equipment along the route; and other technical issues that plagued some of the earliest performances.

    "You have to have the ability to just make it sound decent no matter what," said the music director for "Matilda," David Holcenberg.

    But that means the performers must perfect their lip-syncing while hitting their marks. On Broadway, dozens of "Matilda" cast members weave among, around and over boxes, hopping onto scooters and whipping school coats like scarves as they dance.

    "You've just got to practice," said 11-year-old Bailey Ryon of the Matildas, who said she sometimes rehearses her dance moves while making breakfast.

    Thursday will be bittersweet, the Matilda actresses agreed.

    All four of them will be leaving the show in the next two months, replaced by actresses they have dubbed "Newtildas."

    "It's nice to end with the Macy's Day Parade because it's a very big thing," Milly said. "People will be like, 'Wow.' And then the Newtildas will be inspired."
